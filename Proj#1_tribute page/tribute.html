Dr. Rosalind Franklin

The scientist who paved the way for the double helix theory



File photo of Dr. Rosalin Elsie Franklin(left) and the famous photo-51 depicting the X-Ray diffraction pattern of DNA(right).

Timeline of Dr. Franklin's life:


  • 1919

Born in Notting Hill, London

  • 1926

Started schooling at Norland Place School. Her aunt, Helen Bentwich described: "Rosalind is alarmingly clever â€“ she spends all her time doing arithmetic for pleasure, and invariably gets her sums right."

  • 1938

Passed matriculation, winning a scholarship for University and goes to Newham College, Cambridge.

  • 1941

Earns bachelor's degree, research grant and scholarship under Ronald Norrish at The University of Cambridge.

  • 1942

Accepts invite to work at BCURA (British Coal Utilization Research Association). Her work there helped classify coals and accurately predict their performance for fuel purposes and for production of wartime devices such as gas masks.

  • 1945

Her work in BCURA was the basis of her Ph.D. thesis, The physical chemistry of solid organic colloids with special reference to coal for which Cambridge University awarded her a Ph.D. in 1945.

  • 1946

Stops her work at BCURA after her first scientific papers were published.

  • 1947

Moves to Paris, France to work at the Laboratoire Central des Services Chimiques de l'Etat. She learns about X-ray diffraction techniques under Jacques Mering and becomes an authority on industrial physiochemistry.

  • 1951

Starts work at King's College uses X-ray crystallography techniques on DNA.

  • 1952

Takes perfect photo of the B form of DNA, which she named Photo 51.

  • 1953

  • 30th, Jan - Wilkins, Franklin's colleague, shows James Watson Franklin's photo, Photo 51, without her permission. He also showed Watson all of her reasearch and findings. Watson, Wilkins, and Crick get all of her credit.
  • 1st, Mar  - Leaves King's College and starts X-ray diffraction technique on virus structures at Birbeck college.
  • 7th, Mar  - Crick and Watson publishes discovery of DNA in Journal Nature without crediting Franklin and co-researcher, Gosling.
  • 1st, Dec  - Franklin takes an amazing x-ray of Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV-click here to see the x-ray photograph).

  • 1956

Experiences horrible pains in her abdomen. Doctors discover two large tumors in her ovaries. One tumor is the size of a tennis ball and the other much larger and cancerous. She has them removed.

  • 1957

Franklin's pains return. She is diagnosed with advanced cancer. She begins chemotherapy and continues to work half day at the lab. Franklin begins to work on the polio virus which she believes has great similarities to the yellow turnip mossaic virus. She was right.

  • 1958

Dr. Franklin dies of cancer and sadly a few years after she dies Watson, Crick, and Wilkins won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1962 for all of her hard work, giving her no credit.
